# Comparative In-Silico Analysis of TP53 Mutation Hotspots in Humans and Elephants

## Overview
This repository presents a structured and reproducible computational analysis of TP53 mutation hotspots across human and elephant genomes, with a focus on canonical and retrogene sequences.

The study investigates codon-level conservation of functionally critical TP53 residues associated with human cancers and evaluates their evolutionary behavior in elephants, a species exhibiting reduced cancer incidence (Peto’s paradox).

---

## Scientific Background
TP53 is a central tumor suppressor gene mutated in approximately 50% of human cancers. These mutations are not randomly distributed but cluster at specific codons (R175, G245, R248, R249, R273, R282) within the DNA-binding domain.

Elephants possess multiple TP53 gene copies (including retrogenes), which are hypothesized to enhance genomic stability and contribute to cancer resistance.

This study integrates cancer mutation data with comparative genomics to explore evolutionary constraints at mutation-prone residues.

---

## Research Objectives
- Identify and map canonical human TP53 mutation hotspots
- Perform cross-species comparison with elephant TP53 sequences
- Evaluate conservation across canonical and retrogene variants
- Analyze residue-level variation at structurally critical positions
- Develop a structured computational framework (EleProtect)

---

## Methodology

### Data Sources
- UniProt (Human TP53: P04637)
- NCBI Protein Database (Elephant TP53 sequences)
- COSMIC, TCGA (mutation hotspot data)

### Computational Workflow
1. Sequence retrieval and preprocessing  
2. Protein-level multiple sequence alignment (MSA)  
3. Codon-level hotspot mapping  
4. Residue conservation analysis  
5. Retrogene variability assessment  
6. Exploratory computational scoring  

### Key Features
- Alignment-based positional mapping  
- Domain-aware hotspot interpretation  
- Reproducible workflow architecture  

---

## Key Findings
- Canonical TP53 hotspot residues are highly conserved across species  
- DNA-binding domain shows strong evolutionary constraint  
- Elephant retrogenes exhibit position-specific variability  
- Conservation patterns support functional importance of hotspot residues

- Input sequences  
- Alignment outputs  
- Processed datasets  
- Analysis scripts
